# Example Scenarios and How to Respond

This reference gives fictional customer scenarios and model response patterns for support conversations. Use these for consistency, not as legal advice.

## Auto scenario examples

### Scenario A: Rear-end collision at a stoplight

**Customer context:**  
"I got rear-ended and my trunk is crushed. What do I do first?"

**Recommended response pattern:**

1. Confirm safety and emergency steps.
2. Instruct to exchange information and capture photos.
3. Prompt claim filing channels (app, portal, phone).
4. Explain deductible basics and expected next contact.

**Sample concise response:**  
"I am sorry this happened. First, make sure everyone is safe and call emergency services if needed. Then collect the other driver's details, take photos, and file your claim through the ShieldBase app or our 24/7 claims line. Once assigned, an adjuster will review damage and walk you through repair options and deductible details."

### Scenario B: Windshield crack from road debris

**Customer context:**  
"Can I file this as glass-only damage?"

**Recommended response pattern:**

- Confirm whether comprehensive/glass endorsement may apply.
- Explain deductible outcomes vary by policy and state.
- Suggest same-day claim report and preferred shop workflow.

### Scenario C: Car theft recovery with damage

**Customer context:**  
"My car was stolen, recovered, and now has interior damage."

**Recommended response pattern:**

- Confirm police report number is required.
- Explain comprehensive pathway for theft-related damage.
- Advise documenting recovered condition before repairs.

## Home scenario examples

### Scenario D: Kitchen fire with smoke damage

**Customer context:**  
"Fire was put out quickly, but smoke is everywhere."

**Recommended response pattern:**

- Start with safety and temporary housing question.
- Explain coverage parts potentially involved: dwelling, contents, loss of use.
- Instruct to preserve receipts for emergency expenses.

### Scenario E: Slow leak under sink discovered late

**Customer context:**  
"Cabinet wood is warped from a leak we did not notice."

**Recommended response pattern:**

- Acknowledge situation and gather timeline.
- Explain sudden/accidental vs long-term seepage distinction may affect coverage.
- Encourage immediate mitigation to prevent further damage.

### Scenario F: Storm damage and fallen tree

**Customer context:**  
"Tree fell on my fence and part of my roof."

**Recommended response pattern:**

- Ask if active hazard remains (power lines, structural instability).
- Explain emergency protective repairs are usually prioritized.
- Mention debris removal and structure estimates are reviewed under claim terms.

## Life scenario examples

### Scenario G: New parent reviewing life coverage

**Customer context:**  
"How much term life should I consider with a newborn?"

**Recommended response pattern:**

- Avoid giving personalized financial advice as fact.
- Share general framework: income replacement years, debts, childcare/education goals.
- Recommend licensed advisor follow-up for final amount.

### Scenario H: Beneficiary starting claim

**Customer context:**  
"What documents do I need to start a life claim?"

**Recommended response pattern:**

- Express empathy.
- List typical documents: claim form, certified death certificate, identity verification.
- Explain review timeline depends on completeness and policy conditions.

## Escalation triggers for human support

Escalate to licensed agents or claims specialists when:

- Customer requests legal interpretation of policy language.
- Customer disputes liability or settlement amount.
- Potential fraud indicators appear.
- Catastrophic losses involve displacement or severe injuries.
- Customer asks for tax/estate recommendations beyond general information.

## Voice and guardrails

In chat responses:

- Be clear and calm.
- Avoid guaranteed outcomes before claim review.
- Use "may", "typically", and "depends on policy terms" where appropriate.
- Offer next concrete action in every response.
